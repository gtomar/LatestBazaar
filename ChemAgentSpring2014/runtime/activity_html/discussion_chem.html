Ok, here's the moment of truth! It turns out that the boiling point of stannane is 221 °K, giving it a higher boiling point than both silane and germane. Because these are non-polar molecules, the only intermolecular force at work is the dispersion force. As the molecule at the center of the tetrahedron gets bigger (silane → germane → stannane), so does the molecule's van der Waals area. This means that there is more area over which molecules can experience attractive interactions with other molecules. The increased attraction between molecules results in an elevated boiling point.

Unfortunately, the van der Waals area of germane was not available, so the van der Waals areas of these molecules won't be provided here. However, it turns out that the molar mass of molecules often serves as a helpful proxy for van der Waals area. In general, and certainly within classes of similar molecules, the van der Waals area goes up monotonically with molar mass.

Everything you have learned so far should have prompted you to say that propanol has a boiling point between those of methyl vinyl ether and methyl ethyl ketone. However, it doesn't. Its boiling point is 370.35 °K, which is above the boiling points of both methyl vinyl ether and methyl ethyl ketone. This is true despite the fact that both its dipole moment and van der Waals area fall in between those of the other two molecules. So, what is going on?

Before we get into a formal explanation of this, let's take a look at some more data, just to illustrate that the behavior of propanol is not an anomaly, but rather, part of a systematic trend.

Figure 1: Boiling point as a function of dipole moment for selected ethers, aldehydes, ketones, and alcohols (excludes any branched isomers)

In Figure 1, it's clear that there are two different sets of behaviors. For molecules with the formula CnH2nO – that is, for aldehydes, ketones, and some ethers - an increase in dipole moment from about 1 Debye to nearly 3 Debye results in an a boiling point elevation of 40-50 °K. That's about 20-25 °K per 1 Debye increase in dipole moment.

In contrast, for molecules with the formula CnH2(n+1)O, the alcohols have a boiling point 80-100K greater than their ether counterparts despite a difference in dipole moment of only about 0.4 Debye. Clearly the mechanism responsible for the boiling point behavior of the aldehydes and ketones is not responsible for the alcohols as well.

So, we return to the question, what is going on?

If you have any thoughts on this, share them with your group.

It turns out that hydrogen bonding is a factor here. We haven't discussed hydrogen bonding yet, so let's take a look at it.

Hydrogen bonding comes about when a molecule contains a X-H bond where X is O, F, or N. These highly electronegative atoms pull on the hydrogen's electron such that the hydrogen atom ends up with a partial positive charge. This positive charge, typically denoted δ+, is then attracted to lone pairs of electrons belonging to atoms on different molecules (or even different parts of the same molecule).

Hydrogen bonds can be quite strong (about 1/10th the strength of a covalent bond) and they account for some boiling point behavior that cannot be explained via dispersion or dipole-dipole interactions, like the high boiling point of water.

  • methyl vinyl ether
    propanol
    methyl ethyl ketone

If you look at the molecules in the table above, you can observe that only with propanol, do we have the necessary conditions for hydrogen bonding: an O-H bond. It is this bond and the hydrogen bonding that it enables that is responsible for propanol's unexpectedly high boiling point.

Don't forget, all these forces are cumulative. So, molecules that hydrogen bond with one another also experience dispersion forces and dipole-dipole forces. Recall question 1 about butanol and its branched isomers. The branched isomers had lower boiling points than the non-branched isomer. As discussed, this is because branching caused a reduction in dispersion interactions between molecules. So, even though butanol, like propanol, has hydrogen bonding, part of its boiling point behavior still comes from dispersion forces and if those dispersion forces are reduced, so is its boiling point.

Well, how did you do?

The one consistent trend across all the comparisons is that the van der Waals area increases with each fluorine added. This should have been expected since hydrogen is a small atom.

Hopefully, you also recognized the dipole moment trend. Since fluorine has a high electronegativity, when one fluorine is substituted for one hydrogen, you should expect the dipole moment to go up. When you substitute another fluorine for another hydrogen and you perform the vector sum, you will find that the dipole moment should go up again. When you add a substitute in a third fluorine, the bond dipoles start to cancel out and the dipole moment goes down. Finally, it should have been clear that when you had four fluorines, the bond dipoles completely cancel and the overall dipole moment is zero.

The difficulty here is that the van der Waals area rises with each additional fluorine, while the dipole moment initially increases and then decreases. Given that both the dipole moment and the van der Waals area increase with the substitution of the first two fluorines, it should have been clear that the boiling point would increase. However, when you substitute in a third fluorine and the dipole moment goes down, what happens to the boiling point? Does the effect of the van der Waals area win and increase the boiling point or does the effect of the dipole moment dominate and result in a reduced boiling point? In this case, the dipole moment wins and we see a reduction in boiling point with each of the last two fluorines added. However, it’s worth noting that the boiling point of tetrafluoromethane is higher than that of methane due to the increase in van der Waals area.

Now that we've explained the van der Waals area and dipole moment trends, let's look at the boiling point. In the case of the fluoromethanes, we saw the boiling point track with the dipole moment. What happens here? Well, except for the rise in boiling point from trichloromethane to tetrachloromethane, the boiling point tracks well with the van der Waals area. Why should dipole moment dominate for the fluoromethanes but not the chloromethanes? Well, you can see that, on average, the van der Waals area of the chloromethanes is much larger than that of the fluoromethanes. This means that the dispersion forces will be larger. Evidently, in the case of the chloromethanes, the dispersion forces are large enough that they outweigh the dipole-dipole interactions.

Before we move on to the bromomethanes, you may still be wondering how the boiling point of tetrachloromethane can be higher than that of trichloromethane despite the fact that both its van der Waals area and dipole moment are lower. From the data presented in Table 4, there is no good explanation. However, one possible explanation is that the added chlorine increases the polarizability of the molecule.

If you look at the van der Waals volume of trichloromethane and tetrachloromethane, they are 43.5 cm3/mol and 52.3 cm3/mol, respectively. Since polarizability tracks with van der Waals volume, it seems reasonable, in the absence of any evidence to the contrary, that the added chlorine increases the polarizability of tetrachloromethane. While no polarizability data is available, it seems reasonable to assume that the increased polarizability of tetrachloromethane is sufficiently large so as to offset the reduction in van der Waals area and lead to an increase in dispersion forces. This increase in dispersion forces is then responsible for the increase in boiling point in spite of the reduction in van der Waals area.
